## Top Stories

### Ironwood Lands on Testnet — Mainnet Target: July 21

Zebra 6.0.0-rc.0 shipped July 2 with full NU6.3 "Ironwood" support, activating at testnet block 4,134,000. The new shielded pool is now live on testnet — wallets can route transactions to it, and the Orchard-to-Ironwood turnstile is operational. Ironwood's state DB migrates in-place (no resync from genesis). Mainnet activation is targeted for July 21, though Shielded Labs flagged that exchange, mining pool, and wallet readiness will determine whether the date holds. [Zebra 6.0.0-rc.0](https://github.com/ZcashFoundation/zebra/releases/tag/v6.0.0-rc.0) · [Forum #56521](https://forum.zcashcommunity.com/t/zebra-6-0-0-rc-0/56521)

### New ZCG Composition — Paul Brigner and GGuy Elected

ZCAP election results announced June 29: Paul Brigner (ZODL's Chief Policy & Regulatory Officer) and GGuy (incumbent) take the two open seats, effective July 1. Brigner brings direct DC policy-layer expertise — he's also building PGPZ. This committee will evaluate the largest grant pipeline ZCG has ever faced through H2 2026. [ZF Announcement](https://x.com/ZcashFoundation/status/2071702953757483093)

### KeepKey Files for Orchard Shielded Signing on Hardware

KeepKey submitted grant #341 for full Orchard shielded transaction signing directly on hardware, with integrations into Zingo and ZODL wallets. If approved, KeepKey joins Hito (Milestone 1 submitted for real-device testing) and Ledger's pending Orchard support. The hardware wallet story for shielded Zcash went from nothing to three active efforts in under a year. [GitHub #341](https://github.com/ZcashCommunityGrants/zcashcommunitygrants/issues/341)

Full Ironwood/NU6.3 support. State DB format 28.0.0 with in-place migration. Ironwood note commitment trees via RPC. ## Privacy Corner: The Encryption Precedent

*Inspired by Mastering Zcash by Maxime Desalle — Chapter 5*

In the early 1990s, the U.S. government classified strong encryption as a munition — illegal to export, like a missile. Phil Zimmermann faced a federal investigation for releasing PGP. The argument was familiar: if ordinary people can encrypt their communications, law enforcement goes blind.

The cypherpunks won that fight. Not by proving encryption had no downsides — it does — but by demonstrating that the alternative was worse. A world without strong encryption is a world without secure banking, without private medical records, without e-commerce. Today HTTPS is mandatory on every browser. Nobody argues that your connection to your bank should be cleartext.

Financial privacy is in the same position encryption was in 1993. The objections are identical: "Only criminals need it." "Law enforcement can't function." But infrastructure built on surveillance defaults always loses to infrastructure built on privacy defaults — not because privacy is costless, but because transparency is catastrophically expensive when applied universally.

Zcash is building the HTTPS of money. The question isn't whether it will be adopted. It's how much damage the cleartext era causes before it is.
